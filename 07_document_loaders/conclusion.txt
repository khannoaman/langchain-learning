Conclusion
This thesis investigated the problem of adapting performance prediction models across
heterogeneous environments in edge Function-as-a-Service (FaaS) systems. In decentralized
FaaS (DFaaS) deployments, predictive models trained on one node often experience
performance degradation when applied to other nodes due to differences in hardware
characteristics and workload behaviour. Such discrepancies introduce a distribution shift
that limits the reliability of performance models used by DFaaS Agents for proactive load
balancing and resource management.
To address this challenge, this work explored adapter-based domain adaptation strategies
for DFaaS performance modelling. A unified multi-task architecture was employed to
jointly model resource usage metrics and overload conditions, enabling a single model to
capture multiple aspects of system performance. Lightweight adapter modules were then
introduced to adapt the model to new environments while keeping the base model frozen.
The study systematically investigated different adapter architectures, their placement
within the network, and the amount of target-domain data required to achieve effective
adaptation.

7.1 Answers to Research Questions

The experimental results provide clear answers to the research questions introduced
in Chapter 3. The findings demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed multi-task,
adapter-based framework for DFaaS performance modelling under domain shift.

RQ1 — Unified Multi-Task Modelling
The results demonstrate that a unified multi-task architecture can effectively model both
resource usage prediction and overload classification in the DFaaS context. The proposed
model jointly predicts multiple performance indicators, including CPU usage, memory
usage, and latency, while simultaneously identifying node overload conditions.
Experimental evaluation shows that the multi-task architecture achieves strong predictive
performance across both tasks when trained directly on the target domain. In
particular, the scratch-trained model achieves an overall regression performance of approximately
R2 ≈ 0.83, while maintaining high classification accuracy of approximately
0.96 and a macro-F1 score above 0.90. These results indicate that a shared representation
can successfully capture the relationships between workload characteristics and system
behaviour without requiring separate models for regression and classification tasks.

Overall, the findings confirm that integrating both prediction tasks within a single architecture
is feasible and provides a more efficient alternative to maintaining independent
models.

RQ2 — Adapter-Based Domain Adaptation
The experiments demonstrate that adapter-based domain adaptation effectively restores
predictive performance when a model trained on a source node is applied to a new target
node under distribution shift. When the source model is evaluated directly on the target
domain without adaptation, regression performance deteriorates substantially. The base
model achieves an overall performance of approximately R2 ≈ 0.05, indicating that the
learned workload–performance relationships do not transfer reliably across heterogeneous
nodes.
Introducing lightweight adapter modules significantly improves predictive accuracy.
After adaptation, the best configuration (a bottleneck adapter placed at the pre-output
layer) achieves an overall regression performance of approximately R2 ≈ 0.83, effectively
matching the performance of a model trained directly on the target domain from
scratch (R2 ≈ 0.83). Classification performance also remains strong after adaptation.
The adapted model achieves an accuracy of approximately 0.94 and a macro-F1 score
around 0.88, demonstrating that the adapter-based strategy preserves reliable overload
detection while adapting to the new domain.
These results confirm that freezing the base model and training only lightweight
adapter modules is sufficient to recover most of the predictive performance lost due to
domain shift, while requiring significantly fewer trainable parameters and substantially
shorter training time than full retraining.

RQ3 — Adapter Placement Sensitivity
The experiments show that the position of adapter modules within the network architecture
has a significant impact on adaptation effectiveness. Adapters inserted in early
layers provide only moderate improvements over the base model, indicating that low-level
input representations remain relatively stable across domains.
In contrast, inserting adapters in deeper layers consistently yields stronger improvements
in regression performance. The best results are obtained when the adapter is
placed near the task-specific output layers, particularly in the pre-output position. This
configuration achieves the highest overall regression performance (R2 ≈ 0.83) among the
evaluated placements.
These findings suggest that domain shift primarily affects higher-level relationships
between workload patterns and system performance metrics rather than the initial encoding
of input features. Correcting deeper predictive representations is therefore the
most effective strategy for restoring model accuracy under distribution shift.

RQ4 — Data Efficiency of Adaptation
The data reduction experiments demonstrate that adapter-based adaptation remains effective
even when the amount of available target-domain training data is reduced. Meaningful
performance improvements can be achieved using only a subset of the full dataset.

For regression tasks, stable adaptation performance is maintained with roughly one
third of the original training data, achieving R2 values above 0.73. When the dataset
is reduced further, performance gradually declines but still remains substantially better
than the unadapted base model. For classification tasks, the model shows even greater
robustness to data reduction. Accuracy remains above 0.93 and macro-F1 above 0.85 for
several reduced-data configurations.
However, the experiments also reveal that workload diversity is as important as
dataset size. Filtering strategies that preserve a broader range of workload configurations
produce more stable and reliable results than larger datasets concentrated in
narrow workload regions. This indicates that maintaining representative coverage of the
workload space is essential for effective domain adaptation.
